1. MY TEACHING PHILOSOPHY

AS A SCIENCE TEACHER

I see teaching as more than just a job, but as my responsibility to make a real difference in learners' lives. I believe that teaching is not just transferring knowledge but about helping learners make sense of the world around them. I believe that education opens minds and helps learners to become more thoughtful and responsible citizens.

Core Principles of My Teaching Philosophy:

  • Student-Centered Learning: Education should be tailored to students' needs, interests, and learning styles
  • Growth Mindset: All students can learn and grow with appropriate support and challenge
  • Authentic Assessment: Evaluation should measure meaningful learning and application of knowledge
  • Culturally Responsive Teaching: Education should honor and incorporate students' diverse backgrounds and experiences
  • Lifelong Learning: Education should instill curiosity and skills for continuous learning beyond the classroom

Theoretical Foundations

My teaching practice is informed by several educational theorists and approaches:

PEDAGOGICAL CONTENT KNOWLEDGE (SHULMAN, 1987)

I base my teaching on Shulman's idea of Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK), which means understanding the science content deeply and knowing the best ways to teach it so that learners can understand and grasp even difficult concepts. For example, when teaching forces, I connected the lesson to learners' everyday experiences such as pulling and pushing doors, brushing teeth, and other examples of forces.

CONSTRUCTIVISM (VYGOTSKY, 1982)

I believe that learners build their own understanding by connecting new knowledge to what they already know. Learning happens through experience, reflection, and social interaction, so as a teacher I guide learners to explore, question, and reason together. During my teaching experience, before explaining electricity, I asked learners what they think happens when a bulb lights up, helping them confront misconceptions.

CONCEPTUAL CHANGE

I believe in changing learners' misconceptions about scientific ideas because real learning occurs when learners' old beliefs are replaced with new ones that are scientifically accurate. For example, when teaching electricity, learners thought that electricity gets used up in the circuit, but through experiments they saw that current is the same throughout.

4. LESSON PLANNING

Approach to Lesson Planning

My lesson planning process is systematic, student-centered, and aligned with curriculum standards. I employ backward design principles, starting with clear learning objectives and designing assessments and activities that enable students to demonstrate their understanding.

Key Elements of My Lesson Planning Approach:

  • Clear Learning Objectives: Specific, measurable goals aligned with curriculum standards
  • Engaging Hook: Activities that capture student interest and connect to prior knowledge
  • Scaffolded Instruction: Progressive complexity with appropriate support structures
  • Differentiated Activities: Multiple pathways to achieve learning objectives
  • Formative Assessment: Ongoing checks for understanding throughout the lesson
  • Reflection: Opportunities for students to consolidate and reflect on their learning

Lesson Plan 1: Digitally Rich Environment

Introduction to Simple Circuits and Their Components

Knowledge Strand: Energy and Change
Subject: Natural Sciences
Grade: 8
Duration: 60 minutes

Lesson Objectives

At the end of the lesson, learners should be able to:

  • Define what a simple circuit is
  • Identify the components of a simple circuit (battery, bulb, wire, and switch)
  • Describe each component's function
  • Differentiate between a complete (closed) and incomplete (open) circuit
  • Build circuits using PhET simulations

Resources Required

  • Smartboard
  • Slides
  • Internet connection
  • Simulation: PhET "Circuit Construction Kit -- DC"
  • Video clip: from YouTube
  • Digital quiz (Kahoot)
  • Google Form
  • Tablets
  • Digital worksheet

Accommodation for Diverse Learners

  • Provide slides (images), videos and discussions
  • Allow learners to manipulate simulations
  • Use gamification

Teaching Strategies

  • Inquiry-based learning
  • Demonstration and discussion
  • Simulation-based exploration
  • Digital formative assessment

Knowledge Strand: Energy and Change

Lesson Objectives

  • Define a simple circuit
  • Identify and describe the main components: battery, bulb, wire, and switch
  • Explain the function of each component
  • Differentiate between closed and open circuit
  • Draw a simple circuit

Resources Required

  • Real batteries, bulbs, wires, switches
  • Whiteboard

  • Circuit kits
  • Worksheets and handouts

Accommodation for Diverse Learners

  • Pair struggling learners with stronger ones to help each other more, especially when playing the game

  • Allow learners to draw, write, perform and speak orally to accommodate everyone

Teaching Strategies

  • Using demonstration and guided discussion
  • Gamification
  • Group work between learners as I facilitate
  • Hands-on exploration
  • Observation and reflection

5. ASSESSMENT COMPETENCE

Assessment Philosophy

I view assessment as an integral part of the teaching and learning process, serving multiple purposes including measuring student progress, informing instruction, and promoting student self-regulation. My assessment practices are aligned with curriculum standards and designed to provide meaningful feedback to support student growth.

Principles Guiding My Assessment Approach:

  • Assessment FOR Learning: Formative assessment to guide instruction
  • Assessment AS Learning: Developing students' self-assessment skills
  • Assessment OF Learning: Summative assessment to measure achievement
  • Authentic Assessment: Tasks that reflect real-world applications
  • Differentiated Assessment: Multiple ways for students to demonstrate understanding

Assessment Tasks - Digitally Rich Classroom

Topic: Introduction to Simple Circuits and Their Components

Grade: 8
Duration: Ongoing formative and summative assessment

Assessment Type

Formative: Kahoot quiz and PhET simulation, Digital worksheet and labeled diagram submission

Assessment Task Description
  1. Kahoot Quiz (8 questions)
    • Learners are to complete an interactive digital quiz testing which tests their understanding of circuit components and their functions
    • Example question: "What does a switch do in a circuit?"
  2. Digital Worksheet
    • Learners build and label a simple circuit digitally using PhET simulation
    • Identify components (battery, bulb, wire, switch)
    • Write one sentence describing each component's function
    • Submit via Google Form

My goal is to understand Natural Science concepts much better and be able to explain them in ways that learners will understand. I will focus more in connecting science concepts with learner's everyday experiences, for example when I'm teaching energy transfer, I will use examples they use at home. How am I going to do it?
